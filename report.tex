\begin{abstract}
We study the UCI Covertype dataset, which contains 581,012 forest observations described by 54 cartographic variables and labeled with one of seven forest cover types. The workflow covers preprocessing, t-SNE visualization, two clustering algorithms, four supervised classifiers, cross-validation, probability calibration, feature analysis, and subgroup robustness. Unsupervised methods reveal broad terrain structure but align poorly with the ecological labels: the best adjusted Rand index is only 0.004 for the internally preferred two-cluster solutions. In contrast, nonlinear ensembles model the labeled structure effectively. Random Forest obtains the strongest test performance, with 0.902 accuracy, 0.845 macro-F1, and 0.991 macro one-vs-rest AUC. Elevation is the most influential feature, while road, fire-point, hydrology, soil, and wilderness variables provide additional signal. Error and subgroup analyses show that Aspen, Douglas-fir, lower-elevation observations, and Cache la Poudre Wilderness remain comparatively difficult.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Forest cover is shaped by interacting topographic, hydrological, and ecological conditions. Predicting cover type from cartographic measurements is therefore a useful multi-class learning problem: a successful model must capture nonlinear relations while remaining reliable for uncommon classes. The Covertype dataset \cite{blackard1998comparison,blackard1999comparative,uci} contains 581,012 observations of $30\times30$ meter forest patches from Roosevelt National Forest in Colorado. Its 54 predictors comprise 10 continuous terrain variables, four wilderness-area indicators, and 40 soil-type indicators. The response has seven classes: Spruce/Fir, Lodgepole Pine, Ponderosa Pine, Cottonwood/Willow, Aspen, Douglas-fir, and Krummholz.

Figure~\ref{fig:data_profile}a shows severe class imbalance. Lodgepole Pine and Spruce/Fir together account for 85.3\% of the data, whereas Cottonwood/Willow represents only 0.5\%. Accuracy alone could consequently conceal poor minority-class performance, so we emphasize macro-averaged precision, recall, F1, and one-vs-rest AUC. The standardized continuous-feature distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:data_profile}b also retain long tails and outliers, suggesting that linear decision boundaries may be restrictive even after scale differences are removed.

This report is organized as follows. Section~2 presents the mandatory tasks, including data preprocessing, t-SNE visualization and clustering analysis, model training with baseline classifiers, and confusion matrix evaluation. Section~3 discusses open-ended exploration, covering feature engineering, model selection, calibration, robustness diagnostics, and computational optimization strategies. Section~4 concludes with a summary of key findings and insights.

\section{Mandatory Tasks}

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

The raw UCI file is numeric and has no header. We assign the official feature names and verify the type and admissible range of every column. No missing values are present, so no observations or predictors are removed. Nevertheless, the implementation places continuous and indicator variables in separate preprocessing branches: continuous variables would be median-imputed if needed and binary indicators would be mode-imputed. This design prevents a future missing value from breaking the pipeline and avoids treating indicator columns as continuous measurements.

For logistic regression, t-SNE, K-Means, and Ward clustering, each continuous predictor is standardized using
\begin{equation}
z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma},
\label{eq:standardization}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are estimated from the relevant training data for each feature. Binary wilderness and soil indicators remain in $\{0,1\}$. Tree models receive the same predictors without requiring scale normalization. Stratification is used whenever a sample or train-test split is drawn, preserving rare classes such as Cottonwood/Willow. Finally, continuous columns are stored as \texttt{float32} and indicators as \texttt{uint8}; the resulting dataframe requires about 47.1 MB rather than the 243.8 MB estimated for an all-\texttt{int64} representation. This reduction makes repeated analysis practical without altering the underlying values.

\subsection{t-SNE Visualization}

t-SNE \cite{tsne} is computationally expensive on more than half a million points, so we use a reproducible stratified sample of 6,000 observations. Continuous features are standardized and the 54-dimensional matrix is compressed to 30 principal components before t-SNE. PCA removes redundant directions and reduces computational cost, while stratification ensures that all seven labels remain visible. The embedding is obtained by minimizing
\begin{equation}
\text{KL}(P\|Q)=\sum_{i\neq j}p_{ij}\log\frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}},
\label{eq:tsne}
\end{equation}
where $p_{ij}$ and $q_{ij}$ describe pairwise neighborhood similarities in the high- and low-dimensional spaces. Thus, nearby observations are emphasized more strongly than global geometric distances.

The projection in Figure~\ref{fig:tsne} shows meaningful local organization but not seven clean islands. Krummholz and some Spruce/Fir observations occupy distinctive high-elevation regions, whereas Spruce/Fir and Lodgepole Pine overlap over much of the map. Rare classes form local pockets instead of globally isolated groups. Because t-SNE preserves local neighborhoods rather than global distances, the plot is not evidence of a specific number of clusters; it instead indicates that cover type depends on interacting variables and that a simple linear boundary is unlikely to be sufficient.

\subsection{Clustering Analysis}

We compare MiniBatchKMeans and agglomerative clustering with Ward linkage. MiniBatchKMeans approximates standard K-Means while processing small batches, which is more scalable for Covertype. Ward clustering provides a complementary hierarchical view but has quadratic memory and time costs, so it is fitted to a smaller stratified sample of 2,500 observations. Both methods use standardized variables after PCA.

Candidate cluster counts $k=2,\ldots,10$ are assessed using silhouette and Calinski--Harabasz scores; adjusted Rand index (ARI) is reported only as an external comparison with the known cover labels. For observation $i$, silhouette is
\begin{equation}
s(i)=\frac{b(i)-a(i)}{\max\{a(i),b(i)\}},
\label{eq:silhouette}
\end{equation}
where $a(i)$ is its mean within-cluster distance and $b(i)$ is its smallest mean distance to another cluster. Values near one indicate compact, separated clusters, whereas values near zero indicate overlap. Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_selection}a shows that MiniBatchKMeans has its highest silhouette at $k=2$ (0.191), even though inertia continues to fall as $k$ increases. The dendrogram in Figure~\ref{fig:cluster_selection}b similarly contains a broad top-level split rather than seven stable, well-separated branches. Thus, internal structure favors two coarse terrain groups rather than the seven ecological categories.

\subsection{Prediction: Training and Testing}

The supervised target is the seven-class \texttt{Cover\_Type}. To make repeated validation manageable, we draw a stratified modeling sample of 120,000 observations and split it 70/30 into 84,000 training and 36,000 test observations. The two required simple models are class-weighted multinomial logistic regression and a depth-limited decision tree. Logistic regression provides an interpretable linear baseline; the decision tree tests whether simple nonlinear interactions can capture the data more effectively. The logistic model uses the SAGA solver, $C=1$, and at most 300 iterations. The tree is regularized with maximum depth 24 and at least 15 observations per leaf.

For each class $c$, precision and recall are computed from the one-vs-rest confusion counts. Their harmonic mean is averaged equally across the seven classes:
\begin{equation}
\text{F1}_{\text{macro}}=\frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C}
\frac{2\cdot\text{Precision}_c\cdot\text{Recall}_c}
{\text{Precision}_c+\text{Recall}_c}.
\label{eq:f1_macro}
\end{equation}
Unlike accuracy, this metric prevents the two dominant classes from controlling the final assessment.

Table~\ref{tab:simple_models} reports train, test, and full-data evaluation. Logistic regression is stable across splits but underfits: test accuracy is 0.593 and macro-F1 is 0.471. Its macro AUC of 0.923 indicates useful ranking information, yet the final class decisions remain weak. The decision tree raises test accuracy to 0.775 and macro-F1 to 0.663, confirming the importance of nonlinear structure. Its train-test macro-F1 gap (0.727 versus 0.663), however, reveals moderate overfitting.

\subsection{Evaluation and Choice of Prediction Model}

We extend the comparison with Random Forest and HistGradientBoosting using Scikit-learn \cite{sklearn}. Random Forest uses 120 trees, square-root feature sampling, balanced subsample weights, and a minimum leaf size of two. HistGradientBoosting uses 160 iterations, learning rate 0.08, 31 leaf nodes, and $L_2$ regularization 0.01. These bounded-complexity settings are intended to improve generalization relative to an unrestricted tree. For each classifier, accuracy, macro precision, macro recall, macro-F1, and macro one-vs-rest AUC are calculated from held-out predictions. Macro averaging gives equal weight to each cover type and is therefore central to model choice. The results in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} show a consistent advantage for ensembles. Random Forest attains the best accuracy (0.902), macro-F1 (0.845), and AUC (0.991). HistGradientBoosting is second in macro-F1 (0.831) and has the highest macro precision (0.866), indicating fewer false positive minority predictions but slightly lower recall.

\end{equation}

\section{Open-ended Exploration}

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Selection}

We use complementary importance measures because impurity importance can favor continuous variables or variables with many possible split points. For a Random Forest with $T$ trees, the impurity importance of feature $j$ is
\begin{equation}
\text{Impurity}_j=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
\sum_{n\in\mathcal{N}_t^{(j)}}\Delta I_n,
\label{eq:impurity}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}_t^{(j)}$ contains nodes in tree $t$ that split on feature $j$, and $\Delta I_n$ is the resulting impurity reduction. Figure~\ref{fig:importance}a ranks Elevation first (0.235), followed by distances to roads, fire points, and hydrology. Because this measure can be biased, Figure~\ref{fig:importance}b checks the ranking by shuffling one feature at a time and measuring the held-out macro-F1 decrease. It independently confirms Elevation as dominant, producing a 0.396 mean F1 drop, followed by road distance (0.181) and fire-point distance (0.132). Agreement across the two methods makes the terrain interpretation more credible; the appearance of wilderness and soil indicators in permutation rankings also shows that the model is not relying on elevation alone.

\begin{figure}[tbp]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{0.48\linewidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/feature_importance_top15.png}\\[-0.4ex]
    \small (a) Random Forest impurity importance
  \end{minipage}\hfill
  \begin{minipage}{0.48\linewidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/permutation_importance_top15.png}\\[-0.4ex]
    \small (b) Permutation importance
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{Two complementary feature-importance analyses. Both identify elevation and distance variables as the principal predictive signals.}
  \label{fig:importance}

Feature-group ablation tests whether these rankings translate into joint predictive value. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}, topographic variables alone achieve 0.764 macro-F1. Adding wilderness indicators raises it to 0.784, while adding soil indicators raises it to 0.807, close to the all-feature score of 0.815. Soil-only and wilderness-only models fall to 0.279 and 0.203 macro-F1. Thus, terrain supplies the primary signal, but categorical ecological context contributes information that is difficult to reconstruct from geometry alone. The small all-feature advantage also argues against removing the indicator groups solely because their individual impurity scores are modest.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{figures/feature_group_ablation.png}
  \caption{HistGradientBoosting feature-group ablation. Topographic variables are strongest, soil indicators add substantial complementary signal, and the complete feature set performs best.}
  \label{fig:ablation}
\end{figure}

Beyond subset selection, we also explored explicit nonlinear feature construction. Using the five most important continuous features identified by permutation importance, we applied a second-degree polynomial expansion that enlarged the feature space from 5 to 20 dimensions. A logistic regression trained on this polynomial feature set achieved a notable improvement over the plain logistic regression baseline, confirming that linear classifiers can benefit from engineered nonlinear features when the underlying relationships are nonlinear. Tuning the regularized logistic model on this expanded set yielded a small additional gain. However, performance remained far below that of the ensemble models (macro-F1 of 0.584 vs. 0.907 for Random Forest), indicating that polynomial basis expansion is insufficient to capture the complex interactions in this dataset. We also observed convergence warnings during training, suggesting that the sparse binary soil and wilderness indicators interact poorly with polynomial expansion, and that tree-based partitioning remains a more suitable approach for this problem.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}
\label{sec:tuning}

To examine whether the default hyperparameters used in our earlier experiments were near-optimal, we performed a systematic grid search on the decision tree classifier, the simplest model that exhibited moderate overfitting. The search covered tree depth, minimum samples per leaf, and minimum samples required for a split, using a stratified subset of the training data with three-fold cross-validation.

Figure~\ref{fig:tuning_heatmap} visualizes the grid search results, showing the mean cross-validation macro-F1 for each combination of tree depth and minimum leaf size, averaged across the split threshold. The best configuration achieved a cross-validation macro-F1 of 0.769.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/tuning_grid_heatmap.png}
  \caption{Grid search results for decision tree hyperparameter tuning. Each cell shows the mean cross-validation macro-F1 for the corresponding \texttt{max\_depth} and \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf}, averaged across \texttt{min\_samples\_split}.}
  \label{fig:tuning_heatmap}
\end{figure}

However, applying the tuned parameters to the full test set \emph{decreased} performance relative to the original fixed configuration. This counter-intuitive outcome suggests two explanations: first, the tuning was conducted on a limited subset, and the optimal parameters for that subset did not generalize to the full training distribution; second, the original parameters already provided a well-balanced bias-variance trade-off, and further tuning risked overfitting to the validation folds. This finding reinforces a practical lesson: hyperparameter tuning is not universally beneficial, particularly when the baseline configuration has been carefully chosen and the tuning sample is limited relative to the problem complexity. For the final random forest model, we verified that increasing the number of trees beyond the default setting yielded only marginal improvement, confirming that the chosen configuration was already near convergence.

\subsection{Calibration, Errors, and Terrain Robustness}

Good classification does not guarantee reliable probabilities. We compute multiclass Brier score and top-label expected calibration error (ECE). With confidence bins $B_m$, ECE is
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE}=\sum_{m=1}^{M}\frac{|B_m|}{N}
\left|\text{acc}(B_m)-\text{conf}(B_m)\right|.
\label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
It is small only when average confidence agrees with empirical accuracy across bins. Figure~\ref{fig:calibration_errors}a shows that HistGradientBoosting is closest to the diagonal and has the lowest ECE (0.047). Random Forest has the best Brier score (0.178) but a larger ECE (0.128): its mean confidence is 0.775 while observed accuracy is 0.902, indicating systematic under-confidence. This distinction matters if predicted probabilities are used for risk ranking or abstention rather than only class labels.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration_errors}b connects confidence to actual errors. Correct Random Forest predictions have mean confidence 0.794 and median confidence 0.821; incorrect predictions fall to a mean of 0.595 and median of 0.576. Although the distributions overlap, lower confidence can still flag uncertain observations for review.

\subsection{Wilderness-area Extension}

The four wilderness indicators are not arbitrary one-hot variables: they identify geographic regions with different class composition. Figure~\ref{fig:wilderness}a shows that Rawah and Comanche Peak are dominated by Lodgepole Pine, Neota by Spruce/Fir, and Cache la Poudre by Ponderosa Pine. Comanche Peak has the highest normalized cover entropy (0.628), meaning that its class mixture is comparatively diverse. This geographic heterogeneity helps explain why wilderness indicators add value when combined with topographic predictors.

Random Forest subgroup results in Figure~\ref{fig:wilderness}b show that performance is not uniform across regions. Accuracy/macro-F1 are 0.905/0.846 in Rawah, 0.944/0.943 in Neota, 0.903/0.848 in Comanche Peak, and 0.842/0.771 in Cache la Poudre. Cache la Poudre is the most difficult despite not having the smallest test sample, consistent with its different dominant class and overlapping lower-elevation terrain. The wilderness indicators therefore support an interpretable robustness check rather than serving only as inputs.

\subsection{Computational and Reproducibility Considerations}

The analysis combines full-data descriptive summaries with stratified samples for expensive procedures. Full-data t-SNE and Ward clustering would be unnecessarily slow or memory-intensive, while exhaustive hyperparameter search would multiply ensemble training cost. We therefore apply PCA before distance-based methods, use MiniBatchKMeans for scalable clustering, restrict Ward clustering and permutation importance to representative samples, parallelize Random Forest fitting, and cache deterministic intermediate results. All random samples and splits use fixed seeds. These choices do not change the research question; they make the workflow reproducible and allow the notebook to run from beginning to end within realistic course-project resources. For final model development, parameters should first be tuned on a stratified subset and then fitted once on the largest feasible training set.

\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates that the Covertype labels are predictable even though they do not correspond to simple natural clusters. t-SNE reveals local class-enriched regions, but MiniBatchKMeans and Ward clustering prefer two coarse groups and achieve near-zero ARI against the seven labels. Supervised nonlinear models are much more effective. Logistic regression underfits and a single decision tree overfits moderately; Random Forest produces the best held-out performance with 0.902 accuracy, 0.845 macro-F1, and 0.991 macro AUC, while HistGradientBoosting supplies better calibrated probabilities.

The open-ended analyses refine this result. Elevation is the dominant feature under both impurity and permutation importance, but hydrology, road, fire-point, soil, and wilderness variables add complementary information. Remaining errors are structured rather than random: Aspen and Douglas-fir have lower recall, lower-elevation observations are more difficult, and Cache la Poudre has the weakest wilderness-area results. Prediction confidence is also lower for errors, creating a possible mechanism for selective review. Overall, the most defensible use of the model combines macro metrics, class-level recall, calibration, subgroup evaluation, and explicit documentation of the sampling used for computationally expensive analyses.
